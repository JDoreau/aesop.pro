## The job of the readout

This is not a data dump. It is a **narrative** with a deliberate arc:

> *Here's what we found → here's why it's costing you → here's what to do about it → here's what to decide.*

Three principles:

- **Executive language, not analyst language.** "Five teams define 'active customer' differently and it's why your board numbers don't reconcile" — not "we identified definitional variance across the semantic layer."
- **Lead with the decision, support with the evidence.** Each slide makes a point; the data is there to back it, not to be read aloud.
- **Make them feel the cost before you offer the fix.** The room should agree there's a real problem before you show the roadmap. A solution offered before the pain is felt gets discounted.

> **Format note:** keep it to ~10–12 slides. If a slide doesn't advance the narrative
> arc, it belongs in the appendix, not the readout. The full deliverable package is the
> reference; the readout is the story.

## Slide-by-slide outline

### Slide 1 — Title & frame
**Purpose:** Set the room. Remind them what this was and what they're about to get.
- Engagement name, client, date, "Reporting Clarity Assessment."
- One line: *"What we reviewed, what we found, and what we recommend you do first."*

### Slide 2 — What we did (briefly)
**Purpose:** Establish credibility and scope in 30 seconds. Don't dwell.
- Scope reviewed: N reports/dashboards, N stakeholder interviews, N systems.
- The TRUST method, one line per phase. Signals rigor without a methodology lecture.

### Slide 3 — The headline
**Purpose:** The single most important takeaway, stated plainly. If they remember one slide, this is it.
- One sentence naming the core finding. *"Your reporting isn't a data problem — it's an ownership and definition problem, and it's slowing three decisions a month."*
- Resist hedging. The headline earns attention for everything after it.

### Slide 4 — The current state, honestly
**Purpose:** Show the estate as it is. Make the sprawl/duplication/distrust visible.
- The inventory in one visual: how many reports, how many trusted, how many duplicated, how much manual burden (hours).
- A simple count or chart beats a table here. Let the scale speak.

### Slide 5 — Where trust breaks down
**Purpose:** The heart of the engagement. The high-value / low-trust reports.
- The 3 reports leadership relies on most that they trust least, and why.
- Pull from the Trust & Usage Scorecard. This is the slide that makes the problem feel real.

### Slide 6 — The numbers you argue about
**Purpose:** Make the metric conflicts concrete and personal.
- 1–2 specific KPI conflicts, with the competing definitions and the quantified gap. *"'Active customer' differs by ~8% between Finance and Sales — that's the Q3 board discrepancy."*
- Frame blamelessly: definitions evolved independently, no one's fault, fixable.

### Slide 7 — What it's costing
**Purpose:** Convert findings into business impact. This is where the room decides it matters.
- Time lost to manual reporting (hours → cost). Decisions delayed or made on distrusted numbers. Risk exposure.
- Quantify where credible; estimate transparently where not. Don't oversell — honest numbers land harder than inflated ones.

### Slide 8 — What's working (don't skip this)
**Purpose:** Credibility and balance. An all-negative readout reads as a sales setup.
- The reports/practices that *are* trusted and well-run. What to protect.
- Signals even-handedness — the brand's voice. It also makes the problems more believable.

### Slide 9 — The plan: 30 days
**Purpose:** Show immediate, self-executable momentum.
- The 3–4 highest-impact quick wins they can do themselves in 30 days.
- Each with an owner. This slide says "you have control starting tomorrow."

### Slide 10 — The plan: 60 & 90 days
**Purpose:** The fuller arc — reconcile, standardize, govern.
- The 60-day and 90+ horizons at headline level (the roadmap summary table).
- Name where deeper work is warranted — honestly, not as a pitch.

### Slide 11 — Recommended priorities & next steps
**Purpose:** Leave the room with decisions, not homework.
- The 1–3 things to decide *today* (assign owners, approve the quick wins, schedule the reconciliation).
- If a follow-on engagement genuinely fits, name it plainly here. If the plan is self-executable, say that.

### Slide 12 — Appendix pointer
**Purpose:** Close cleanly; point to the full package.
- "Full inventory, scorecards, conflict map, source map, and detailed roadmap in the deliverable package."
- Contact line. Thank the sponsor and interviewees by role.

## Delivering it in the room

- **Send nothing in advance** unless asked — present live so you control the narrative arc, then send the deck + full package after.
- **Watch the sponsor on slides 5–7.** That's where you learn whether the problem landed. If they're nodding, the roadmap will sell itself.
- **Don't read slides.** Each slide is a visual anchor for a point you make out loud. If you're reading it, it has too many words.
- **End on the decision, not the deck.** The last words should be "so the three things to decide today are…" — not "any questions?"
- **Leave the conversion conversation for after the decisions.** If a Sprint or Governance Kit fits, raise it once they've absorbed the findings and agreed on priorities — never before.

> **The readout succeeds when** leadership leaves saying *"we knew some of this was a
> problem, but now we know exactly what it is and what to do first."* That clarity — not
> the slide design — is the product.

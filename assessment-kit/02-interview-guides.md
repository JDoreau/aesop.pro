# Reporting Clarity Assessment — Stakeholder Interview Guides

> **Aesop Analytics · TRUST Method, Phases 1–2 (Triage, Reconcile)**
> Three role-based guides. Conduct 3–6 interviews total, 30–45 minutes each.
> The point is not to read every question — it is to surface where trust breaks down,
> where definitions diverge, and which reports actually drive decisions.

## Interview principles

A few things that make these interviews produce usable findings rather than a transcript:

- **Listen for the gap between what is built and what is used.** The most-built report and the most-trusted report are often not the same one. That gap is the engagement.
- **Ask for the specific, not the general.** "Reporting is a mess" is not a finding. "Finance and Sales both report 'active customers' and the numbers differ by ~8%" is a finding. Always push from the abstract to the concrete example.
- **Capture the exact words people use for metrics.** Conflicting definitions usually hide inside identical vocabulary. Write down how each person *defines* a term, not just that they used it.
- **Distinguish five failure types as you listen** — note which one each complaint really is:
  design, data quality, definition, ownership, or adoption. The same symptom ("I don't trust this dashboard") has different fixes depending on the type.
- **Stay neutral on people.** When a stakeholder blames a team or a tool, record the friction without adopting the blame. Ownership gaps are structural, not personal.
- **One question at a time. Then stop talking.** The silence after a question is where the real answer comes from.

> **Recording & notes:** Ask permission to take notes (and to record, if your SOW and
> their policy allow it). Tell interviewees their input will be synthesized and anonymized
> in the findings — candor depends on it.

## Guide 1 — Executive Sponsor / Leadership

*Who: COO, CFO, VP, Controller, Program Director — the person who feels the pain and owns the outcome.*
*Goal: understand the decisions at stake, the cost of the current state, and what success looks like.*

### Opening
- In your own words, what made you decide to bring someone in for this now?
- If this engagement goes perfectly, what is different in 90 days?

### The decisions
- Walk me through a recent decision where the reporting made it harder, not easier.
- Which numbers does your leadership team argue about? What does the argument usually sound like?
- Which report or dashboard do you personally open most often? Do you trust it?
- Is there a number you quietly double-check or recalculate yourself before you act on it?

### The cost of the current state
- How much time does your team spend assembling reporting versus acting on it?
- What happens — to the business, to a decision, to the board — if nothing changes?
- Has a reporting problem ever led to a wrong or delayed decision you can describe?

### Scope & priorities
- Of everything we could look at, what matters most to *you*?
- Is there anything political we should know — a report someone is attached to, a definition that's contested, a team sensitivity?
- Who else must we talk to for this to be credible?

### Close
- If you could wave a wand and fix one reporting thing tomorrow, what would it be?

## Guide 2 — Report Builders / Analysts

*Who: the people who actually build the dashboards and spreadsheets — analysts, BI developers, the "Excel wizard" everyone relies on.*
*Goal: map how reporting is actually produced, where it's fragile, and where definitions live (or don't).*

### How reporting actually gets made
- Walk me through how a typical recurring report gets produced, start to finish.
- Which reports are fully automated, and which still need manual assembly each cycle?
- Where does data come from for the key reports? Which source do you treat as authoritative — and why that one?
- What breaks most often? What's the thing you hold your breath about each month?

### Definitions & logic
- When you calculate [name a key metric the sponsor mentioned], how exactly is it defined? What's included and excluded?
- Has anyone ever disagreed with one of your numbers? What turned out to be the cause?
- Where do the metric definitions live — in documentation, in your head, in the formula?
- If you were out for a month, could someone else reproduce your reports? What would trip them up?

### Manual burden & fragility
- Roughly how many hours per week or month go to manual reporting work that you'd automate if you could?
- Which spreadsheets are load-bearing — the ones that would cause real problems if they broke?
- Where do you copy-paste between systems? Where does a human have to "fix" data by hand?

### Tools & duplication
- Are there multiple versions of the same report? Do you know which one people actually use?
- What gets built and then ignored? What do people ask for that already exists somewhere?

### Close
- What would make your job meaningfully easier that nobody has asked you about?

## Guide 3 — Department Heads / Report Consumers

*Who: the managers and team leads who consume reporting to run their part of the business but don't build it.*
*Goal: understand trust, usage, and the gap between what's delivered and what's needed.*

### What they actually use
- Which reports do you rely on to run your area? How often do you look at them?
- Which ones do you ignore, and why?
- When you need a number that isn't in a standard report, what do you do?

### Trust
- Is there a report you use but don't fully trust? What makes you uneasy about it?
- Have you ever been in a meeting where two people had different numbers for the same thing? What happened?
- When you get a number, do you know where it came from and who stands behind it?

### Gaps & friction
- What question about your area can you *not* currently answer with the reporting you have?
- How long does it take to get a new report or a change to an existing one? Is there a process, or do you just ask someone?
- If a report is wrong, who do you tell — and does it get fixed?

### Close
- What's the one report or number you wish you had that you don't?

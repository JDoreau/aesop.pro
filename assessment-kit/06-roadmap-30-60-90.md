## What this document is

The roadmap is what the client is actually paying for. The other nine deliverables are
the evidence; this is the answer to *"so what do we do about it?"* It must be:

- **Prioritized** — sequenced by impact and dependency, not listed alphabetically.
- **Owned** — every item has a named owner (a role if not a person yet). Owner-less items don't happen.
- **Honest about effort** — small / medium / large, so leadership can plan capacity.
- **Self-executable** — the client can act on the 30-day items without Aesop. That's the promise: the Assessment stands alone.
- **A bridge, not a leash** — where deeper work is warranted, it names that plainly (and that's where Sprint / Governance / Retainer conversations begin), but it never manufactures dependency.

> **The framing that lands:** open the readout roadmap with one sentence —
> *"Here is what to fix in the next 30 days yourself, what to plan for the next 60, and
> what to decide about for the 90+ horizon."* Leadership hears control, not a sales pitch.

## How items are scored

Each roadmap item carries five attributes. Keep them consistent so the plan reads as one
coherent system, not a pile of recommendations.

| Attribute | Scale / values | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Impact** | High / Medium / Low | On trust, decision speed, or risk. Tie back to a specific finding. |
| **Effort** | Small / Medium / Large | S = days, M = weeks, L = a project. Be honest — credibility depends on it. |
| **Owner** | Role or name | Who is accountable. "Unassigned" is a flag to resolve at the readout. |
| **Dependencies** | Item IDs / systems / decisions | What must happen first. Drives sequencing. |
| **Risk if skipped** | One line | What continues to cost them if this isn't done. |

**Sequencing rule:** within each horizon, order by impact first, then by whether other
items depend on it. A low-effort/high-impact item with no dependencies is always a 30-day
quick win. A high-impact item that everything else depends on may need to start in the
30-day window even if it's large.

## Where this leads (conversion paths — handle with integrity)

If the 60/90 horizon contains work the client can't realistically do alone, say so plainly
and map it to the right next engagement. This is not upselling — it's telling them the
truth about what the findings require. If they *can* do it themselves, say that too.

| If the findings show… | The honest next step is… |
|---|---|
| One high-value reporting package needs a real rebuild | **Executive Reporting Modernization Sprint** |
| Ownership/standards keep slipping; no operating rhythm | **Data Governance Starter Kit** |
| They need ongoing senior oversight, not a one-time fix | **Fractional Analytics Operations Retainer** |
| Definitions are now trusted and they want to move on AI | **AI-Ready Analytics Assessment** |
| The 30/60/90 plan is genuinely self-executable | **Nothing further — and say so.** That honesty is the brand. |

> **Guardrail:** never inflate the roadmap to manufacture a follow-on engagement. A client
> who can execute independently and is told so becomes a referral source. A client who is
> oversold becomes a cautionary tale. The promise — *"we bring you value, or we tell you
> honestly that we can't"* — is enforced right here.

Dashboard Design & Modernization

Chart choice is a question
about the data relationship,
not the data.

Most people pick charts by habit or imitation. A better approach: identify the relationship your data contains, then match the form to it. A practical framework for the six relationships that show up in business dashboards — and the common choices that quietly fail.

By Jonathan Doreau, Aesop Analytics • Dashboard Design & Modernization • 9 min read

A bar chart is the default. Someone needs to show sales by region — bar chart. Revenue by quarter — bar chart. Headcount by department — bar chart. The bar chart is not wrong for any of these, but it's also not necessarily the best choice for all of them. It's just the one everyone reaches for first.

The problem isn't that people choose bad charts. It's that they choose without a framework. They match the chart to a vague sense of what "looks right" rather than to the specific claim the data needs to make. The result is dashboards where every panel technically displays data but some of it lands clearly and some of it doesn't — and the people building the dashboard can't always explain why.

There's a more reliable path. Every dataset contains a relationship — a structural fact about how the numbers relate to each other or to time. Identify that relationship first. Then the chart choice follows from it, rather than from convention or aesthetics.

The six relationships — and the charts that serve them

In business dashboards, data relationships fall into six categories. Most metrics you'll ever need to visualize map cleanly to one of them. A few span two, and when that happens you have a genuine design decision to make — but at least you're making it consciously.

Relationship 1

Comparison

"How do these items rank against each other?"
You have discrete categories and you want to show their relative magnitude. The categories are independent — you're not showing how they add up to a whole, just how they stack up against each other.
Use Bar chart Horizontal bar Dot plot Avoid Pie chart Radar chart
Relationship 2

Trend over time

"How is this changing, and in what direction?"
You have a continuous time axis and you want to show movement. The continuity matters — you're not comparing discrete points, you're showing a path between them.
Use Line chart Area chart Sparkline Avoid Bar chart (many periods) Dual-axis line
Relationship 3

Part-to-whole

"What share of the total does each piece represent?"
The parts add up to a meaningful whole and you want to show proportions. The constraint: this only works clearly with a small number of categories (ideally 2–5) and when the proportional differences are large enough to be visible.
Use Pie chart (≤5 slices) Donut chart 100% stacked bar Avoid Pie chart (>5 slices) 3D pie
Relationship 4

Distribution

"How is this value spread across a range?"
You want to show the shape of the data — where most values cluster, where the outliers are, how spread out the range is. This shows up less often in executive dashboards but is essential in operational and analytical views.
Use Histogram Box plot Violin plot Avoid Line chart Bar chart
Relationship 5

Correlation

"Do these two variables move together?"
You have two continuous variables and you want to show whether they tend to rise and fall in the same direction (or opposite directions). This is a hypothesis about a relationship, not a confirmation of causation.
Use Scatter plot Bubble chart Avoid Line chart (two series) Dual-axis bar
Relationship 6

Flow & composition over time

"How does the makeup of this total change as it grows?"
You want to show how the proportional breakdown of a total shifts over time — not just the overall trend, but how the mix within it is changing. The hardest relationship to visualize well and the most commonly mishandled.
Use Stacked area (few series) Small multiples Avoid Stacked bar (many periods) Stacked bar (many series)

The charts that consistently fail — and why

The framework above tells you what to use. But some charts are so commonly misused that they deserve a direct treatment. These are the choices that seem reasonable, appear in professional tools with prominent placement, and quietly undermine the dashboard that contains them.

Chart Why it fails Use instead
Pie chart with >5 slices Human perception can't reliably compare arc angles. Small slices become invisible or misleading. The legend becomes the actual chart. Horizontal bar chart — length is the easiest dimension to compare accurately.
Dual-axis line chart Two independent scales on one chart invite the viewer to see a relationship that may not exist. The correlation appears to change depending on how you set the axis ranges. Two separate charts with clear labels, or a scatter plot if you're explicitly testing correlation.
Stacked bar with many series Only the bottom series is readable — every other segment floats on a shifting baseline, making comparison impossible. People think they're seeing composition; they're actually seeing noise. Small multiples (one bar chart per series) or a 100% stacked bar if proportional breakdown at each point is all that matters.
3D charts of any kind The third dimension adds no information and distorts the proportions of the first two. A 3D pie chart makes the front slices look larger than the back slices regardless of their actual values. The flat equivalent of whatever you're trying to show. Always.
Bar chart for time series (>12 periods) A bar chart implies discrete comparison, not continuous movement. When you have more than ~12 periods, the bars become thin, the labels overlap, and the trend is harder to read than it would be on a line. Line chart for the trend; reserve the bar chart for a period-level comparison view when someone wants to compare specific months.

When the relationship is ambiguous

Sometimes a dataset contains two relationships simultaneously, and both are legitimate. Revenue by region, trended over time — is that a comparison or a trend? The answer depends on what question matters most to the person looking at it.

If the primary question is "which region is performing best?" — that's a comparison question. Show a ranked bar chart of total revenue per region, with a period selector if trend context is needed.

If the primary question is "are all regions growing?" — that's a trend question. Show a small multiples line chart: one panel per region, same scale, so trends are directly comparable without stacking them into a single cluttered chart.

The wrong move is to try to answer both questions simultaneously — typically with a grouped bar chart across 12 months and 6 regions, which produces 72 bars and answers nothing clearly. When in doubt, split the view. Two focused charts beat one ambitious one.

The one-sentence test: Before finalizing any chart, write the sentence it's supposed to make obvious. "Revenue in Q3 was higher than Q2." "The West region is underperforming all others." "Customer acquisition cost has been rising steadily since March." If you can't write that sentence in under ten seconds, the chart isn't doing its job — regardless of how it looks.

A note on the bar chart

After all of this, the bar chart is still the right choice for a surprising number of situations. It's reliable because it uses length — the dimension human perception evaluates most accurately. It handles comparisons, rankings, single-period distributions, and discrete time periods competently. Its dominance in business dashboards is partly habit, but it's also partly deserved.

The problem isn't that people use bar charts too often. It's that they use them without asking the question — and that same unreflective habit is what leads them to reach for pie charts when they want to show composition, or dual-axis lines when they want to show two trends, or stacked bars when they want to show six things at once.

The framework isn't about avoiding the familiar. It's about making the familiar choice consciously, having ruled out the alternatives for a reason, rather than arriving at it by default.

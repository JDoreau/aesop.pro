Dashboard Design & Modernization

A dashboard is a claim.
Make it deliberately.

Most dashboards are bloated because nobody made the hard editorial calls. Before you think about layout or color, you need a framework for deciding what earns a place on screen — and what belongs somewhere else.

By Jonathan Doreau, Aesop Analytics • Dashboard Design & Modernization • 8 min read

I've looked at a lot of dashboards. The ones that don't work — the ones people ignore, distrust, or quietly stop opening — share a common problem. They weren't designed. They were accumulated.

Someone needed revenue. Someone needed headcount. Someone needed ticket volume. Someone needed days-to-close. Each request made sense in isolation. Nobody said no. The dashboard became a warehouse: everything technically available, nothing clearly prioritized, no implied point of view about what actually matters.

The fix people reach for is usually cosmetic — better charts, new colors, a redesign. But the underlying problem isn't visual. It's editorial. A good dashboard is a claim about what matters to a specific person making a specific set of decisions. Until you've made that claim explicitly, any amount of visual polish is just decoration on a problem you haven't solved.

The one question every dashboard metric has to answer

Here's a useful forcing function: before any metric earns a place on a dashboard, it has to pass a single test.

If this number changed significantly overnight, would the decision-maker need to know before their next meeting?

That's it. Not "is this data useful?" Not "does someone care about this?" Those bars are too low — they describe almost every metric in your data warehouse. The question is whether this person, in this role, making these decisions, would need to act or adjust based on a significant change in this number.

If the answer is yes — it belongs on the dashboard. If the answer is "maybe eventually" or "it's good context" or "the CFO likes seeing it" — it probably belongs somewhere else: a report, a drill-down, a weekly digest, a shared spreadsheet. Not on the primary screen that's supposed to tell you what needs attention right now.

The three-question framework

That single test is a useful gate, but it doesn't tell you how to organize what passes through it. In practice, dashboard metrics do one of three things. If a metric isn't doing one of these three things clearly, it shouldn't be on the dashboard.

The Three Questions a Dashboard Metric Must Answer
1
Status
"Where do we stand right now?"
Current pipeline value, open tickets, active customers, cash on hand. These are the vital signs — the numbers that tell you whether the system is functioning within normal parameters.
2
Trend
"Is the direction right?"
Revenue week-over-week, churn rate over 90 days, support volume month-to-month. A single number tells you where you are. A trend tells you whether you're moving toward or away from where you need to be.
3
Alert
"Is something wrong that I don't know about yet?"
Metrics near a threshold, outlier performance in a segment, a leading indicator moving in the wrong direction. These are the early-warning lights — not always urgent, but worth surfacing before they become urgent.

Notice what's not on that list: context, history, completeness, comprehensiveness. All of those are legitimate analytical needs. None of them are dashboard needs. A dashboard is a monitor, not an archive.

What doesn't belong — and where it actually goes

The practical challenge isn't knowing the framework. It's having the conversations required to enforce it. Every metric that gets cut from a dashboard has a sponsor — someone who wanted it there, someone who'll notice its absence. The key is not to say "this data isn't important." It is important. It just belongs somewhere else.

Dashboard — answers status, trend, or alert
  • Current vs. target for the team's primary KPI
  • Week-over-week trend on a leading indicator
  • Any metric with a defined threshold or SLA
  • Numbers that change daily and drive daily decisions
  • Red/yellow/green status on active commitments
Not the dashboard — belongs elsewhere
  • Historical comparisons beyond one or two periods
  • Segment breakdowns (go deeper on click, not on load)
  • Metrics that change monthly, reviewed in monthly reports
  • Data that informs strategy, not daily operations
  • Vanity metrics nobody acts on but everyone likes seeing

"Belongs elsewhere" is not a dismissal — it's a routing decision. Segment breakdowns belong in a drill-down or a secondary page. Historical comparisons belong in a quarterly review deck. Monthly metrics belong in a monthly report. The point isn't to hide data; it's to surface the right data for the right moment so decision-makers don't have to search for signal in a wall of numbers.

The audience problem

One reason dashboards become warehouses is that they're built for everyone simultaneously. An executive, a frontline manager, and an analyst all need different things from the same underlying data. The executive needs to know if the business is on track. The manager needs to know which team members or accounts need attention. The analyst needs to understand why.

These are three different dashboards — or at minimum, three different views. Trying to build one that satisfies all three produces something that serves none of them well. The executive's screen gets cluttered with operational detail. The manager can't find the specific signals they need. The analyst can't dig into anything without going back to the source.

A practical test for audience clarity: Write one sentence describing the person who will look at this dashboard every morning. Include their role, their primary decisions, and what a bad day looks like for them. If you can't write that sentence — or if writing it reveals you're actually thinking about two or three different people — you don't have a dashboard yet. You have a specification problem.

The editorial discipline nobody wants to do

Once you have the framework and a clear audience, the actual work is cutting. This is the part most organizations avoid. It requires someone with enough authority to say "that metric is important, but it doesn't belong here" — and enough conviction to hold that line when the pushback comes.

In my experience, the conversations are almost always the same. The CFO wants the gross margin on the revenue dashboard. The VP of Sales wants competitor win rates on the pipeline dashboard. The Head of Support wants sentiment scores on the ticket dashboard. Each of these is real data. Each of them has a legitimate place somewhere. The question is whether that place is the primary screen their team opens every morning.

The answer is usually no — not because the data isn't valuable, but because it doesn't change the decisions that need to be made at that level on a daily basis. A gross margin number that moves meaningfully once a quarter doesn't belong on a daily revenue monitor. Win rates that require a month of data to be statistically meaningful don't belong on a weekly pipeline view.

Every metric on a dashboard is implicitly telling its audience: this is worth your attention right now. Make sure you mean it.

Start with a blank screen

The most useful exercise I've found when auditing an existing dashboard is also the simplest: pretend it doesn't exist yet. Start with a blank screen and ask the intended audience — not the data team, not the requester, the actual daily user — what three to five things they want to know when they open their laptop in the morning.

Almost always, the answers are simpler than what's currently on screen. They want to know if they're on track for the month. They want to see what's on fire. They want a quick read on whether the team is moving in the right direction. They don't ask for segment breakdowns, comparative period analysis, or operational detail — unless something in those top-line numbers prompts a specific question.

That's your dashboard. Everything else is one click away.

The goal isn't a minimal dashboard for its own sake. Simplicity is the outcome of disciplined curation, not an aesthetic preference. A dashboard that shows six things, all of which answer status, trend, or alert for a specific person making specific decisions, is doing its job. One that shows thirty because nobody wanted the hard conversation isn't — no matter how clean the design looks.
